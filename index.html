How it works

How we calculate tranche size

The calculator starts with position sizing — how much of your total portfolio should go into one stock. This depends on how many stocks you own:

  • 1 stock: You can allocate 30–50% of your portfolio
  • 2–3 stocks: Allocate 15–30% each
  • 4+ stocks: Allocate 10–20% each

Once you know your max position size, the tranche formula kicks in: Dip percentage × Money you have to spend = Tranche size.

Here's why: If a stock drops 6% and you have $100 to invest, you'd add $6. This ties your spending directly to how far the stock fell—bigger dips = bigger opportunities. We cap it at 20% of the position's current cost so you don't accidentally overweight one stock. The tranche—a measured portion you buy on each dip—can't push your total position above your personal maximum. That's your safety ceiling.

What is a tranche?

A tranche is a measured portion of money you spend on a single dip. Rather than investing all your cash at once, you buy in smaller chunks as the price falls. This way, you never accidentally go all-in on one bad day, and you lower your average cost over time.

What is dry powder?

Dry powder is cash you keep in reserve—money you haven't invested yet. It's your "ammunition" for buying when dips happen. A good rule: keep 10% of your portfolio in dry powder at all times, so you're always ready to buy quality stocks at better prices.

What is cost basis?

Cost basis is the total amount of money you've spent on a position so far. If you bought 100 shares of a stock at $50 each, your cost basis is $5,000. If you later buy 50 more at $45 each, your new cost basis is $7,250 (for 150 shares).

What is deworsification?

Diversification is smart, but over-diversification is 'deworsification'—you end up with more positions than you can properly track, diluting your returns. (This term was coined by Charlie Munger, a famous investor and Warren Buffett's longtime business partner at Berkshire Hathaway). Munger calls for a concentration of individual stock positions you really understand rather than have a lot of different positions for the sake of having a lot of different positions. Before you invest in any company, you need to have a strong thesis, or reasoning, behind investing in the company. The company needs to be one you really understand. Also, it should be one you can keep up with as events and changes happen that could alter your thesis about it.

When to skip a dip (even with a green light)

The calculator might say "Add $X," but there are times to hold back:

  • The stock keeps falling after you buy (deploy smaller, wait for stabilization)
  • Earnings are coming up in 5 days (higher uncertainty)
  • Your thesis has weakened (re-run your analysis first)
  • Insiders are selling (a potential red flag)
  • Your dry powder would drop below 10% after the buy (preserve your cash floor)

This is a framework, not advice

This calculator is educational only. It shows you one approach to thinking about position sizing. You make the final decision. Always run your own analysis, understand your thesis, and only invest money you can afford to lose.
